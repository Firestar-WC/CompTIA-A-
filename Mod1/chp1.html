Laptop Hardware and Components

Hardware Device Replacement

  • Anything involving swapping out hardware deals exclusively with laptops.
  • However, it is not the same steps when it comes to troubleshooting them, because of the manufacturer make or model.

Keyboard

  • Most of the time, help in through two spots: a screw at the bottom of the case and a connector that looks like a cable.
  • They generally snap in when in the right spot.
  • Always organize your screws with labels.

Hard Drive

  • Similar to installing memory; do not have to take laptop apart to get to hard drive bay
  • Remove battery (Safety Concern!)
  • Unscrew, remove old hard drive, snap in new one, replace any connections, rescrew

Hard Drive Types and Sizes

  • There are three types of disks:
    • Magnetic Disk
    • Solid State Drive (SSD; looks like a little flash drive)
    • Hybrid
  • SSD doesn't have cylinders (magnetic disk does), is much faster, is virtually indestructible, decreases boot time, but is quite expensive!
  • The hybrid versions of these drives store only the most accessed data in the SSD part of the drive.
  • Know what your bay size (the slot for the hard drive) is before buying one (2.5 in or 1.8 in for laptops).

Memory

  • Remove battery, unscrew the memory cover (in some cases, at the bottom of the laptop), take out old chip, replace with the new chip, rescrew and reattach all connections
  • Most people think the chip goes in straight down, but it actually goes in at an angle,

Smart Card Reader

  • Some laptops have space for 2 drives; The SSD takes the place of a CD or DVD player that is built in.
  • Make sure the size is right.
  • Batteries are easily replaceable (usually lithium ion, or Li+)
  • Pay attention when disassenbling to get to a part; use a phone to take photos of all of the different placements and connections from the components to each other or the motherboard.
  • The smart card reader is kept near or under the palm rest.
  • When disassembling, work from back to front.

Optical Drives and Wireless Cards

  • Optical drives can be snapped back into place (usually with screws).
  • Wireless cards are generally connected to the LCD (careful!)
  • External ones are easy to connect, rather than internal ones, but pay the price by not being as strong or not giving as good quality as internal ones.

Cellular and Video Cards, Mini PCIe

  • External cellular cards plug into a laptop, giving internet connectivity through a provider; hardly ever needed or used nowadays.
  • Video cards, common in high-end laptops, require making sure there is an available slot and installing the driver.
  • Mini PCIes give extra slots to hold video cards and other stuff.

Other Laptop Parts

  • To replace a screen, pop off the old one and put the new one on. Remember, the wireless antenna is often attached to it.
  • If you aren't recieving power even when the battery is plugged in, and the battery works just fine, then the DC jack might need replacing. Just unscrew it and rescrew it.
  • Touchpads are hard to replace, but are hardly ever needed. They are very durable, and usually the other parts of the laptop give out before it does.
  • If the plastic frame of the computer is broken, then it is generally safe to assume that many other internal parts are broken as well. Another sign to replace it!
  • When replacing spakers, check the sound cards first. Speakers are hardly ever the issue.
  • For replacing system boards (motherboards) or CPUs, remove the old one and replac with the new one, making sure that all connections to it are re-established.
  • Make sure the integrated peripherals are in the same spot for system boards!
  • One thing to remember: system boards are meant to be reliable and don't usually break. If it crashes somehow, it might be better to get a new laptop rather than remove all the parts and replace it.
